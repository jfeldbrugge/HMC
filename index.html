Hamiltonian Monte Carlo

Job Feldbrugge

The Hamiltonian or Hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC) method is an elegant way to sample nontrivial distributions in probability theory based on physical intuitions. It was developed by algorithm was originally proposed by Simon Duane, Anthony Kennedy, Brian Pendleton and Duncan Roweth in 1987 for calculations in lattice quantum chromodynamic (see the paper). These notes serve as a brief introduction to the core of the HMC method for sampling distributions, inspired by the great review by Michael Betancourt.

The classification of surfaces asserts that every connected closed compact surface is topologically homeomorphic to either a sphere, a sphere with a number of handles or crosscaps. This theorem has been known since the 1860’s, but was recently simplified by John H. Conway (1992), known as the Zero Irrelevance Proof or in sort the ZIP proof. I will here describe a beautiful topological proof for the classification of combinatorial surfaces. These notes are based on combinatorial topology of surfaces by Robert C. James (1955), Conway’s ZIP proof by Francis and Weeks (1999), algebraic topology by Norman Wildberger available on Youtube, and the wikipedia page.

We start by defining combinatorial surfaces and illustrating the construct with several examples.

Definition: A combinatorial surface is a finite set of \(n\)-gons where every edge identified with a direction and a label, such that every label occurs twice.
